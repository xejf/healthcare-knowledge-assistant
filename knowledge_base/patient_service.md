# Patient Service Workflow Guide

*Fictional sample document for the Sunrise Valley Health Clinic training project.*

## Greeting Patients

- Acknowledge every patient within 30 seconds of arrival, even if you are busy: a smile and "I'll be right with you" is enough.
- Use a warm, professional greeting: "Good morning, welcome to Sunrise Valley Health Clinic. How can I help you today?"
- Never discuss a patient's personal or health details where other visitors can hear. Invite them to step closer or to a private area if needed.
- For check-ins: verify identity, confirm appointment details, collect any co-pay, and let the patient know the approximate wait time.

## Handling Complaints

1. Let the patient explain fully without interrupting.
2. Acknowledge their feelings: "I understand this is frustrating, and I want to help."
3. Apologize for the inconvenience without assigning blame: "I'm sorry this happened."
4. Fix what you are authorized to fix immediately (e.g., correct an appointment error).
5. For anything outside your authority, explain the next step and who will follow up.
6. Document the complaint in the feedback log the same day.

Never argue with a patient, dismiss a concern, or promise outcomes you cannot guarantee.

## De-escalation Basics

When a patient is angry or distressed:

- Stay calm; keep your voice low, slow, and steady.
- Use their name if known, and maintain relaxed, open body language.
- Listen and reflect back: "So the main issue is the long wait — is that right?"
- Offer clear choices instead of refusals: "I can't change that charge myself, but I can connect you with Billing right now or take a callback request."
- If the patient remains very upset, becomes verbally aggressive, or you feel unsafe, involve your supervisor immediately. For threats or physical aggression, follow the security protocol and alert security at extension 2911.

## Professional Communication

- Use plain language; avoid clinic jargon and abbreviations with patients.
- Never speculate about medical matters. If a patient asks a clinical question, say: "That's a great question for your care team — I'll make sure they know you'd like to discuss it."
- Keep personal opinions about providers, treatments, and other patients to yourself.
- On the phone: answer within three rings when possible, give your name and department, and always ask permission before placing a caller on hold.

## Documentation Expectations

- Record every significant patient interaction (complaints, unusual requests, escalations) in the clinic system before the end of your shift.
- Stick to facts: what the patient said, what you did, who you involved. Do not record opinions or assumptions.
- Never store patient information in personal notes, phones, or outside the clinic system.
- If you make a documentation mistake, follow the correction procedure — never delete or hide the original entry.
