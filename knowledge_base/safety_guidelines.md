# Safety and Quality Guidelines

*Fictional sample document for the Sunrise Valley Health Clinic training project.*

## Reporting Safety Issues

All staff are responsible for reporting safety concerns — no issue is too small.

1. If there is immediate danger (fire, violence, medical emergency in the waiting area), follow the emergency protocol first: alert the on-duty charge nurse and, if needed, security at extension 2911.
2. For non-urgent hazards (wet floors, broken equipment, blocked exits, faulty doors), place a visible warning if possible and report through the online Incident Report Form within 2 hours.
3. For near-misses (something almost went wrong), submit an incident report as well — near-miss reports help prevent future harm.
4. Reports are non-punitive: staff are never penalized for reporting in good faith.

If you are unsure whether something counts as a safety issue, report it anyway or ask your supervisor.

## Privacy Reminders

- Patient information may only be accessed when you need it to do your job.
- Never discuss patient details in hallways, elevators, break rooms, or on personal devices or social media.
- Lock your screen every time you step away from a workstation (Windows key + L).
- Position screens and documents so visitors cannot read them.
- Shred printed material containing patient information in the designated shredding bins — never regular trash.
- Report any suspected privacy breach to your supervisor and the Privacy Officer (extension 2700) immediately. Fast reporting reduces harm.

## Avoiding Unauthorized Advice

Non-clinical staff must never provide:

- Medical diagnosis or interpretation of symptoms or test results.
- Treatment, medication, or dosage advice of any kind.
- Opinions on whether a patient should seek or skip care.
- Insurance coverage or approval determinations.
- Legal advice.

Safe phrases to use instead:

- "I'm not able to advise on that, but your care team can — let me help you reach them."
- "That's a question for our Billing Department; I'll connect you."
- "I don't want to give you inaccurate information, so let me get the right person."

Providing unauthorized advice can harm patients and is a serious policy violation, even when the intention is helpful.

## When to Contact a Supervisor

Contact your supervisor promptly when:

- You are unsure about any procedure or policy and the knowledge base does not cover it.
- A patient interaction escalates beyond routine handling.
- You notice a safety, privacy, or quality concern involving another staff member.
- A patient or visitor reports symptoms that could indicate an emergency.
- Anyone asks you to bypass a documented procedure.

When in doubt: pause, don't guess, and ask. Escalating early is always the right choice.
